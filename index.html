Rehearsal

Why a rehearsal matters without a coordinator: A rehearsal typically takes 20–30 minutes and covers only movement — who walks where, when, and in what order. It does not require a full ceremony read-through. The single most important outcome is identifying who leads the cue at the back of the ceremony space. Without that person rehearsed and confident, the processional stalls.

One person must lead — not a committee. The officiant is ideal if available. Otherwise the point person, best man, or maid of honor. The leader on the day before should be the same person cuing walkers on June 13.

Only people with a role in the ceremony need to attend: the couple, officiant, all wedding party members, parents walking or being escorted, and anyone giving a reading.

If skipping a formal rehearsal, the couple must distribute a written processional order to every participant at least 2 days before, and the cue person must individually confirm with each walker what signal they will use.

Minimum no-rehearsal plan: Written processional order delivered to every participant by June 11. Cue person calls or texts each walker the day before to walk through their specific moment. Music operator has a written cue sheet. Officiant has a written ceremony order. All four of these things together can substitute for a rehearsal at a small wedding.

Photography — when and what

The central photography decision: Whether the couple does a first look before the ceremony determines the entire photo timeline. With a first look, most formal portraits happen before the ceremony and the couple can enjoy cocktail hour with guests. Without a first look, couple portraits happen during cocktail hour and guests entertain themselves for 45–60 minutes. Neither is wrong — but the decision must be made now so the photographer can build the shot timeline accordingly.

A first look allows couple portraits, wedding party photos, and most family formals to happen before the ceremony. The tradeoff is giving up the traditional aisle reveal. Note: research consistently shows grooms still react emotionally at the aisle even after a first look.

Industry guidance: limit formal groupings to ~15 or fewer. Each grouping takes 5 minutes. More than 15 will consume your entire portrait window. List every combination you want (example: bride + groom, full wedding party, bride + bridesmaids, groom + groomsmen, bride + her parents, groom + his parents, both families together...).

Stepping away for 10–15 minutes during dinner or early dancing for golden hour portraits is one of the most recommended moves for summer weddings. Guests are occupied with food and conversation — they rarely notice the couple is gone briefly.

This is the single most common cause of portrait delays. Without one person whose only job is gathering family groups, portraits run 15–20 minutes over. Assign a specific person — not the photographer.

Guest experience during portraits — cocktail hour plan

The cocktail hour reality check: If there is no first look, guests will be on their own for 45–60 minutes after the ceremony while portraits are taken. With a first look, guests have 30–45 minutes of cocktail hour. Either way, guests need something to do beyond standing with a drink. For a small 50-person wedding without a DJ or coordinator, the key is having 1–2 activities that encourage mingling and feel intentional — not a full entertainment program.

Check all that apply. The bar, snacks, and music are the baseline. Activities are optional but strongly recommended if portraits will run longer than 30 minutes.

Practical recommendations for this wedding:

  • Best low-effort, high-impact option: Giant Jenga or cornhole plus snacks. Lawn games rent as a package for $150–$400, require no setup expertise, work for all ages, and give guests a natural conversation starter.
  • Best zero-cost option: Wedding trivia cards printed at home — questions about the couple that guests fill out and drop in a box. Winner announced at dinner. Creates conversation and gives guests something to do at their seats.
  • Best keepsake option: Disposable cameras on tables. Guests take candid photos all evening. Developed after the wedding for a second set of informal images the photographer did not capture.
  • DIY photo booth: A decorated backdrop (a doorway with flowers, a banner, a blank wall) and a sign with a wedding hashtag or message. Guests take photos on their own phones. Costs almost nothing.
  • If outdoors: S'mores station with a fire pit and a kit ($1.50–$2.00 per guest) — one of the most memorable and photographable additions for a summer Idaho evening.

Even 10 minutes with guests during cocktail hour — a quick hello and a drink together — significantly improves guest experience and gives guests a moment with the couple before the formal reception begins.

Water and non-alcoholic beverages

Quantities for 50 guests over 8 hours with open bar and summer heat:

  • Water: Plan for 3–4 bottles (16.9 oz) per guest, or ~200 bottles total. For a warm June day in Idaho, lean toward 4 per person. Buy in bulk at Costco or Sam's Club (~$4–5 per 40-pack).
  • Soft drinks (soda/sparkling): ~26 liters or 74 cans for 50 guests over 8 hours. Include cola, diet cola, and lemon-lime at minimum. These also serve as bar mixers.
  • Ice: Approximately 1–1.5 lbs per guest for beverages alone = 50–75 lbs. Add more if keeping drinks cold in coolers. Buy day-before or morning-of to prevent melting.
  • Pitchers or dispensers: 2–3 large pitchers or a water dispenser on each table. Refillable dispensers are more eco-friendly than individual bottles and look better on tables.
  • Cups: 100+ cups minimum — guests lose cups, grab new ones, use multiple throughout the night. If the bar provides cups, confirm they cover non-alcoholic service too.

Confirm this with the bartender. If the bartender only handles alcohol, someone else must manage the soft drink and water service throughout the event.

This is one of the most consistently forgotten details at weddings. Couples are pulled in every direction during dinner service and often do not eat despite being the ones who ordered the food. A named person whose job is to bring the bride and groom a plate is a simple fix.
